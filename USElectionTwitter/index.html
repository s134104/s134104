History of Tweets

This study spans over a period of roughly 4 months, more specially from July 13th 2016 to November 10th 2016. This repesents the period from one month after the presidential primaries and caucuses to 2 days after the Election Day.


During this period, more than 1,000,000 tweets have been collected, cleaned, filtered, sorted, and otherwise processed. Of particular importance, 6,444 tweets have been collected from the candidates themselves and 199,456 tweets from the election day itself.

The top 10 hashtags are extracted from this data, and is represented in the streamgraph below.

This streamgraph above attest to how (much) each of the top 10 hashtags change over time - including highlights for each of the 3 presidential debates. It is particularly interesting how Donald Trump has managed to maintain a strong and constant adaptation of the hastag and slogan #MAGA (Make America Great Again) throughout the entire election period - this is in strong contrast to Hillary Clinton, whose campaign slogan #ImWithHer has a fairly lower representation throughout. Moreover, 5 out of 10 hashtags are related to the Trump campaign, whereas only 3 is for the Clinton campaign. The remaining 2 hashtags are #Election16, and the anti-Trump hashtag #NeverTrump. However, the cumulative representation for the Trump campaign is much stronger during the entire election period. The empty period from August 16 to August 28 is due to missing data.

A Wordcloud representation for all the tweets originating from either Trump or Hillary is seen below. This illustration can be interpreted as which words defined the discourse during the election for each candidate:


From these wordclouds, it is clear that Clinton attempts to appeal to her followers' pathos, using words like family, country and people, as well as focusing on women. Trump has chosen a different approach, focusing on jobs, personal attacks with crooked (from #crookedHillary), as well as thanking a lot of people.

The wordsclouds provides only part of the whole picture. From these tweets, a sentiment analysis has been conducted on the candidates' tweets. This essentially shows how positively or negatively loaded their tweets have been throughout the election period. The following Calendar View shows the sentiment (or 'tone') on each day for each candidate. The mismatch between the two calendars is due to when each candidate started using Twitter:
It is interesting to see how the most positive of days for Clinton is when she receives endorsements from e.g. Barack Obama (POTUS) or Michelle Obama (FLOTUS). The rest of the days, her tone has been fairly evened out by both positive and negative tweets.
Clinton's calendar view is in stark contrast to Trump's, which shows much more variation. This is in part because of the many harsh personal attacks on Clinton, scare-tactics, and a lot of positivity, appreciation and 'thanks' for others.

Twitter Landscape

This election has exhibited the lowest voter turnout in almost 20 years [1]. This is reflected in how many tweets originated from each state, indicating which states cared most about the election enough to tweet about it. This is illustrated in the US state map visualization below:


It can be seen that the normalized percentages never exceeds 5%. More interestingly, only 2 swing states, Nevada & Florida, made an - albeit very small - effort to express themselves on Twitter during this election. This is surprising, given how swing states typically determine the election.

It is also worth noting that the west coast, east coast, and southern border states have the strongest showing. This is consistent with some of the discourse found in the tweets, e.g. the topic of immigration. These coast and border states takes in most immigrants [2], which could prompt these states to address this online. Immigration and foreign policy is one of the biggest topics this election [3].

Such correlations between states and topics will be further explored in the next section.


Candidates v. Voters

These visualizations have culminated in the correlation between candidates, voters, and topics. This section will explore which topics were most important to each state and candidate and their influence on the election. The top topics during the election has been put up against the states and candidates in the following parallel coordinates:

The parallel coordinate at the top illustrates which issues were most important for the voters in each state, while the bottom parallel coordinate illustrate how much the candidates addressed these issues. These parallel coordinates can be filtered based on political party affiliation. It is worth noting that the Swing States are more aligned with the Republicans than with Democrats, however this might fall within the margin of error.

Interestingly, Clinton's spread of tweets are more consistent with the voters' than Trump is. This follows the trend of Trump's general discourse, which tends to be more 'aggressive' than the norm on certain issues. For instance, Trump rates higher on immigration than both Clinton and the voters.

These diagrams show that the Economy played a far bigger role on Twitter than the rest of the issues. It is followed by Taxes, with Immigration, Education, and Corruption being close seconds for the voters. For Clinton, the top issues were Economy, Taxes, and Education. Unsurprisingly, Trump's top issues are Economy and Immigration. With the results of the election known, and based on these diagrams, an argument can be made that it is not simply enough for the candidates to align themselves with the voters tweets (populism), as is evident with Clinton. Rather, it is important to cut through the noise and outspeak the norm on key issues.
